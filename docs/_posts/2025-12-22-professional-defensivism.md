    <h1 class="packet-title-main">Defensive Professionalism</h1>
    <h2 class="packet-title-sub">The quiet cousin of defensive medicine</h2>

    <div class="hn-grid">
      <!-- MAIN -->
      <div class="hn-main">

        <p class="packet-p">
          We talk a lot about <strong>defensive medicine</strong> — decisions driven not by what’s best for the patient,
          but by what is safest for the doctor or the system.
        </p>

        <p class="packet-p">
          What we talk about far less is that the same incentive pattern shows up across other professions — including law.
          Not incompetence. Not bad intent. But decisions optimized primarily for <strong>institutional self-protection</strong>,
          even when that narrows outcomes for the client.
        </p>

        <div class="hn-anchor">
          <div class="label">Working definition</div>
          <div class="hn-quote">
            Defensive professionalism is when recommendations optimize for <strong>defensibility</strong> first — what can be justified if everything goes wrong —
            rather than what best serves the client in context.
          </div>
        </div>

        <h2 class="section-heading hn-h2">What it looks like in practice</h2>
        <ul class="hn-list">
          <li>Narrow the problem early</li>
          <li>Segment issues instead of integrating them</li>
          <li>Avoid escalation even when the facts support it</li>
          <li>Keep the work inside the safest lane (process, policy, department, practice group)</li>
          <li>Prefer what’s defensible internally over what’s optimal externally</li>
        </ul>

        <p class="packet-p">
          None of this is inherently unethical. In many systems, it’s rational. But it carries a real cost:
          <strong>leverage, optionality, and outcomes narrow quietly</strong>.
        </p>

        <hr class="hn-hr" />

        <h2 class="section-heading hn-h2">Why it happens</h2>
        <p class="packet-p">
          This dynamic shows up anywhere the downside risk is asymmetric and reputational:
          medicine, law, engineering, architecture, finance, cybersecurity, and yes — accounting.
        </p>

        <p class="packet-p">
          The professional often controls the frame. Once a conservative frame is chosen, alternatives disappear,
          escalation becomes “unnecessary,” and the client may assume the outcome was inevitable. Often, it wasn’t.
          It was selected because it was safer.
        </p>

        <div class="hn-anchor">
          <div class="label">The question worth asking</div>
          <div class="hn-quote">
            Not just <em>what</em> is being recommended — but <strong>why this framing</strong> was chosen, and <strong>whose risk</strong> it primarily protects.
          </div>
        </div>

        <p class="packet-p">
          Defensive medicine is widely acknowledged. Defensive professionalism is everywhere — just quieter.
          Seeing it early can make all the difference.
        </p>

      </div>
